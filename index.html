The Problem

nothing financial.

ESG ratings aren't financial models
MSCI, Sustainalytics, and S&P ESG scores measure disclosure quality. They cannot tell you EV at risk under a 2°C pathway — which is exactly what TCFD and IFRS S2 now require.

Regional buckets miss asset reality
Two facilities in the same country can have 10× different flood exposure based on elevation and coastal distance. Regional scores mask the differences that actually drive insurance pricing and capex decisions.

EU, UK and APAC regulators now require scenario analysis with quantified financial impact, transition risk metrics, and physical hazard data — not narrative statements.

Methodology

How the engine actually works.

Four computational layers convert raw asset data into regulatory-grade climate risk numbers. No black box — here is every step.

LAYER 1
Site Physics

Every asset's GPS coordinates are matched against satellite elevation data and climate hazard grids. This matters because two factories in the same city can face completely different flood risk depending on whether one sits on a floodplain. We use WRI Aqueduct 4.0 for water stress, NASA NEX-GDDP for temperature projections, and IPCC AR6 warming trajectories to score 25 physical hazards per site — spanning chronic hazards (heat stress, water stress, drought, sea-level rise, permafrost thaw) and acute hazards (extreme heat events, riverine and coastal flooding, tropical cyclones, wildfires, extreme precipitation, landslides, saltwater intrusion, and more) — calibrated from real-world event loss data.

LAYER 2
Hazard → Money

Hazard scores are translated into USD figures. Physical risk becomes an expected annual production loss (USD/year) and a required adaptation capex budget. Transition risk is computed by applying NGFS Phase 4 carbon price paths to the company's Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions — yielding a carbon cost trajectory through 2050 under three scenarios: Net Zero 2050, Delayed Transition, and Current Policies. Each scenario produces its own EBITDA compression estimate.

LAYER 3
Financial Quantification

Loss costs and carbon costs feed into a discounted cash flow model. A climate-adjusted WACC is computed using regulatory capital adjustment factors, and a 25-year DCF runs for all three scenarios simultaneously. The output is enterprise value under each pathway and an NPV impact percentage — how much firm value is at risk if carbon policy tightens. This is the number that matters to lenders and equity analysts, and the one that goes directly into IFRS S2 quantitative disclosures.

LAYER 4
CRI Composite Rating

The three pillars are weighted and combined into a single 0–100 score: Physical Risk 30% + Transition Risk 40% + Financial Impact 30%. Transition receives the largest weight because carbon policy repricing is the primary near-term financial risk for most sectors. The score maps to a letter rating: A (0–20, low risk) through E (81–100, critical). Higher score means more risk — consistent with how credit rating agencies treat ESG risk factors.

Analytical Engine

The CRI Engine is written entirely in Python — the standard language for scientific computing, climate modelling, and financial analysis. Python lets us use the same tools that climate scientists and quant analysts use: NumPy for hazard calculations, Pydantic for strict data validation, and standard DCF math that any analyst can audit.
